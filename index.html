Problem 1 – Sound and the Sprint Start

In a 100 m race, two sprinters run in lanes whose centers are separated sideways by \(3.66\,\text{m}\). The starter’s pistol is fired near lane 1. Assume:

  • Both sprinters start exactly when they hear the gun.
  • The speed of sound is \(343\,\text{m/s}\).
  • In the official result, Runner A finishes in \(9.784\,\text{s}\), Runner B in \(9.789\,\text{s}\) (so A wins by \(0.005\,\text{s}\)).

(a) If they instead had to react to the real gunshot (no speakers), by how much earlier would the closer runner hear the sound than the farther runner?

(b) Using that time advantage, which runner would win, and by roughly how much time, assuming their running speeds stay the same as in the original race?

Hint: (a) \( \Delta t = \dfrac{\Delta d}{v_\text{sound}} \), where \( \Delta d = 3.66\,\text{m} \). (b) Compare that hearing-time difference to the original \(0.005\,\text{s}\) win margin.

Problem 2 – Stressed Rail

A long steel rail is held rigidly so that it cannot expand. Steel has:

  • Young’s modulus \(E = 195\,\text{GPa} = 1.95\times10^{11}\,\text{Pa}\),
  • Linear expansion coefficient \( \alpha = 1.63\times10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1} \),
  • Maximum allowed stress \( \sigma_{\max} = 600\,\text{MPa} = 6.0\times10^{8}\,\text{Pa} \).

If the temperature increases by \( \Delta T \), the rail “wants” to expand, but the constraints prevent it, so stress builds up instead.

Question: What maximum temperature change \( \Delta T_{\max} \) (in K) can the rail withstand before the stress reaches \( \sigma_{\max} \)?

Hint: For free expansion \( \dfrac{\Delta L}{L} = \alpha \Delta T \). For elastic stretching \( \sigma = E \varepsilon \), where \( \varepsilon = \dfrac{\Delta L}{L} \). Combine to get \( \sigma = E \alpha \Delta T \Rightarrow \Delta T = \dfrac{\sigma}{E\alpha} \).

Problem 3 – Shadow Walking

A street lamp is a point light source at height \( H = 3.2\,\text{m} \). A person of height \( h = 1.8\,\text{m} \) walks directly away from the lamp along flat ground at constant speed \( v = 1.5\,\text{m/s} \).

Let:

  • \( s(t) \) = horizontal distance from lamp to the person at time \( t \),
  • \( x(t) \) = length of the person’s shadow on the ground behind them,
  • The person starts at some initial distance \( s_0 \) at \( t = 0 \).

(a) Using similar triangles, express \( x \) in terms of \( s \), \( H \), and \( h \).

(b) Write \( s(t) \) and then \( x(t) \) explicitly as a function of \( t \).

(c) What is the acceleration of the tip of the shadow? (The tip is at distance \( s(t) + x(t) \) from the lamp.)

Hint: Similar triangles give \( \dfrac{s + x}{H} = \dfrac{x}{h} \Rightarrow x(s) = \dots \). The person moves at constant speed \( s(t) = s_0 + vt \). Decide whether the tip’s position is linear, quadratic, etc. in \( t \).

Problem 4 – Thumb-Rule Plane Spotting

Martin looks out the window and sees a passenger plane crossing his field of view. He uses the rule:

“1 thumb at arm’s length = \(2.5^\circ\) of angle.”

He estimates:

  • The plane stays in view for 10 s.
  • During that time, it sweeps an angle of 7.5 thumbs across the sky.
  • So the angular distance it travels is \(7.5 \times 2.5^\circ\).
  • The plane itself appears about half a thumb long, and he guesses it’s a Boeing 737-800 of actual length \( 40\,\text{m} \).

Assume the plane flies in a straight line perpendicular to Martin’s line of sight (across his view).

Question: Estimate the plane’s speed in km/h based on these numbers.

Hint: Convert thumbs → degrees (and then radians). Use \( \dfrac{\text{length}}{\text{distance}} \approx \) angular size (in radians), then total path length \( \approx \dfrac{\text{angular travel}}{\text{angular size}} \times (\text{length of plane}) \), and finally \( v = \dfrac{\text{distance traveled}}{\text{time}} \).
